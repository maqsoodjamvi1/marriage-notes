If you feel like you and your spouse are roommates who just happen to share a Netflix password, you aren’t alone. Many couples hit a point where the conversation is limited to logistics: “Did you pay the electric bill?” or “Who is picking up the kids?”

When the romantic spark dims, we often look for grand solutions. We think we need a week-long vacation or a massive, heart-to-heart conversation to fix everything. But my experience, and the observations of many who have navigated these waters, suggest that the health of a marriage isn’t built on grand gestures. It is built on “bids.”

What is a Bid for Connection?

A bid is any attempt from one partner to another for attention, affirmation, affection, or any other positive connection. It can be as small as a sigh, a pointed finger at a bird outside the window, or a question about your day.

When your partner says, “Wow, look at that sunset,” they are making a bid. They aren’t just commenting on the weather. They are saying, “I want to share this moment with you. Do you value my perspective? Are we still a team?”

The way you respond to these bids—either by turning toward them, turning away, or turning against them—determines the emotional bank account of your relationship. If you consistently turn toward these small moments, you build a reservoir of goodwill. If you ignore them or snap at them, the account goes into overdraft.

How to Recognize the “Bids” You Are Missing

We often miss bids because we are distracted by our phones, our work stress, or our own internal monologue. If your spouse asks, “Do you think we should try that new Thai place?” they aren’t just asking about dinner. They are inviting you into a shared experience.

To start noticing these, try a 24-hour experiment. Don’t try to change your relationship yet. Just observe. When your spouse speaks to you, ask yourself: Is this a bid for connection?

  • The “Look at this” bid: They show you a funny meme or a news article.
  • The “Can you help me?” bid: A request for a minor favor that could be done independently.
  • The “I’m tired” bid: A heavy sigh or a physical gesture that invites comfort.
  • The “Remember when” bid: A comment about a shared past memory, looking for validation.

The Art of “Turning Toward”

Turning toward a bid means acknowledging it with positive attention. You don’t have to drop everything you are doing for an hour. You just have to acknowledge that the person standing in front of you matters more than the screen in your hand.

Here is how to practice this in real-time, even if things currently feel distant:

  • The Pause: When you are working or scrolling and your spouse speaks, physically turn your torso toward them. Stop typing. Close the laptop lid or put the phone face down. That physical movement sends a signal that they are the priority.
  • The Elaboration: Don’t just grunt or say “cool.” Ask a follow-up question. If they show you a picture of a dog they saw, ask, “Was it a golden retriever?” Even a three-second question shows you were listening.
  • The Physical Touch: A hand on the shoulder or a quick squeeze on the arm while you acknowledge their comment creates a chemical connection that words alone cannot achieve.

When You’re Too Annoyed to Respond

Sometimes, your partner makes a bid and your internal reaction is immediate irritation. Maybe you feel they aren’t pulling their weight, or you’re still simmering from an argument yesterday.

When this happens, it is tempting to “turn against”—to make a snide comment or roll your eyes. This is the fastest way to kill a marriage. If you don’t have the emotional capacity to be warm, choose “neutral” over “negative.” A simple, “I’m feeling a bit overwhelmed right now, can we talk about this in ten minutes?” is a much better response than a dismissive grunt. It protects the relationship while honoring your current limits.

Small Shifts, Not Big Conversations

One of the most common mistakes in a struggling marriage is forcing “the big talk.” These conversations often lead to circular arguments and defensiveness. Instead, focus on the low-stakes environment.

Try these three specific steps this week:

  • The Morning Launch: When you wake up, spend 30 seconds asking something other than a chore-related question. “What’s one thing you’re looking forward to today?”
  • The Re-entry: When you see each other after work or daily tasks, put your phone in a drawer for the first five minutes. Acknowledge their presence before you start talking about the house or the schedule.
  • The Appreciation Bid: Make your own bid. Don’t wait for them. Point out something small—a song you like, a compliment about something they did—and see how they respond. If they turn toward you, validate that connection.

If you find that your attempts to “turn toward” are consistently met with indifference, or if you find it impossible to even try because of lingering resentment, it may be time to consider whether your current patterns have become too deeply ingrained to untangle alone. Identifying these small, daily habits is the foundation for any deeper work you might choose to pursue.

As you start testing these small interactions, you will likely notice a change in the temperature of your home. It won’t happen overnight, but these small, consistent efforts are what prevent a marriage from drifting into total silence. Once you start noticing the “bids” that occur every day, you can move toward more intentional ways to rebuild that sense of partnership.
